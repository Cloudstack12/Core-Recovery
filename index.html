EMDR Therapy Definition:
A New Approach To Trauma Treatment



In recent years, the mental health field has seen the rise of a new and innovative approach to trauma treatment – Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) Therapy. EMDR Therapy Definition refers to psychotherapy that helps individuals process and heal from traumatic experiences. Developed by Dr. Francine Shapiro in the late 1980s, EMDR Therapy gained recognition as an effective treatment for Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) and other trauma-related mental health conditions.

In this blog post, we will delve into the definition of EMDR Therapy, its brief history, and its purpose as a new approach to trauma treatment. We will also explore the benefits of EMDR Therapy and its effectiveness in treating various types of trauma.

What Is Emdr Therapy

EMDR therapy is a psychotherapy designed to help individuals process and overcome traumatic experiences. Trauma can be defined as any event that causes a significant psychological or emotional disturbance, such as abuse, assault, accidents, or natural disasters. EMDR therapy utilizes a structured approach involving standardized protocols and techniques that enable individuals to reprocess traumatic memories and experiences in a safe and controlled environment.

EMDR therapy involves bilateral stimulation, alternating stimulation of the left and right sides of the brain. This can be achieved through various methods, including eye movements, hand tapping, or auditory tones. The bilateral stimulation facilitates the reprocessing of traumatic memories and helps individuals to form more adaptive and healthy beliefs about themselves and the world.

How Does Emdr Therapy Work

The exact mechanism of how EMDR therapy works is not fully understood, but it is believed to involve integrating traumatic memories and experiences with more adaptive and positive information. During an EMDR therapy session, individuals are asked to recall a traumatic memory or experience while engaging in bilateral stimulation, such as following the therapist’s finger movements with their eyes. This helps to activate the brain’s natural healing processes and allows individuals to reprocess the traumatic memory more adaptively.EMDR therapy involves a structured approach that consists of eight phases. These include history-taking, preparation, assessment, desensitization, installation, body scan, closure, and re-evaluation. Each stage has specific goals and objectives and is designed to facilitate the processing and integration of traumatic memories and experiences.

Who Can Benefit From Emdr Therapy

EMDR therapy treats many trauma-related disorders, including post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), depression, anxiety, and panic disorders. It is particularly effective for individuals who have experienced single-incident trauma, such as car accidents or natural disasters, and those who have experienced repeated trauma, such as childhood abuse or neglect.EMDR therapy can be used with individuals of all ages, including children and adolescents, and can be tailored to meet the specific needs of each individual. However, it is essential to note that EMDR therapy may not be suitable for everyone, particularly those with a history of psychosis or severe dissociation.

Limitations Of Emdr Therapy

Although EMDR therapy effectively treats trauma-related disorders, it is not a panacea and may not work for everyone. The success of EMDR therapy depends on various factors, including the severity and complexity of the trauma, the individual’s readiness and willingness to engage in the treatment, and the therapeutic relationship between the individual and the therapist.EMDR therapy may not be appropriate for individuals currently in crisis or at risk of harming themselves or others. In these cases, other forms of treatment, such as crisis intervention or hospitalization, may be necessary.

The Emdr Therapy Process

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) therapy is a unique and practical approach to treating trauma-related disorders. The therapy involves a structured process that includes eight phases designed to help individuals reprocess traumatic memories and experiences in a safe and controlled environment. This article will explore the EMDR therapy process, including the eight phases, the role of the therapist and the patient, and what to expect during a therapy session.

The Eight Phases Of Emdr Therapy


History-Taking: During this initial phase, the therapist collects information about the individual’s history, including any past trauma, medical history, and current symptoms.

Preparation: The therapist works with the individual to establish a therapeutic relationship and provides them with education and coping skills to help manage the distress that may arise during the therapy process.

Assessment: The therapist helps the individual identify the specific target memories or experiences they want to reprocess and work on.

Desensitization: In this phase, the individual recalls the target memory or experience while engaging in bilateral stimulation. This helps activate the brain’s natural healing processes and allows the individual to reprocess the memory more adaptively.

Installation: The therapist helps the individual integrate positive and adaptive beliefs about themselves and the world into the reprocessed memory.

Body Scan: The therapist helps the individual scan their body for residual physical sensations or emotions related to the target memory or experience.

Closure: The therapist helps the individual to feel stable and grounded after the therapy session and provides them with additional coping skills and support as needed.

Re-evaluation: The therapist evaluates the progress of the therapy and makes any necessary adjustments to the treatment plan.

The Role Of The Therapist In Emdr Therapy


The therapist plays a crucial role in the EMDR therapy process. They are responsible for guiding the individual through each phase of the therapy and providing them with support and feedback. The therapist helps the individual to identify target memories or experiences, develop coping skills, and integrate positive and adaptive beliefs about themselves and the world. The therapist also facilitates bilateral stimulation and helps the individual process the traumatic memories in a safe and controlled environment.

The Role Of The Patient In Emdr Therapy


The individual seeking EMDR therapy plays an active role in the therapy process. They are responsible for identifying target memories or experiences, recalling the memory while engaging in bilateral stimulation, and integrating positive and adaptive beliefs about themselves and the world. The individual also plays a crucial role in establishing a therapeutic relationship with the therapist and developing coping skills to manage any distress that may arise during the therapy process.

What To Expect During An Emdr Therapy Session


During an EMDR therapy session, the individual will work with the therapist to identify a specific target memory or experience to reprocess. They will then recall the memory while engaging in bilateral stimulation, which may involve following the therapist’s finger movements with their eyes, hand tapping, or auditory tones. The therapist will help the individual to reprocess the memory in a safe and controlled environment, integrating positive and adaptive beliefs about themselves and the world. The therapist will also help the individual manage any distress that may arise during therapy.

Scientific Evidence Of Emdr Therapy

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) therapy is a relatively new approach to treating trauma-related disorders. While some have questioned this therapy’s efficacy, a growing body of scientific evidence suggests it can be an effective treatment for trauma. In this article, we will explore the scientific evidence supporting EMDR therapy, how it differs from other trauma treatments, and the controversies surrounding this therapy.

Numerous research studies have been conducted on EMDR therapy, including randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses. These studies have consistently shown that EMDR therapy effectively treats trauma-related disorders such as post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), anxiety, and depression.

For example, a meta-analysis conducted in 2018 reviewed 26 randomized controlled trials of EMDR therapy and found that it was more effective than other trauma-focused therapies in reducing PTSD symptoms. Another study published in the Journal of Traumatic Stress found that EMDR therapy was effective in reducing symptoms of anxiety and depression in individuals who had experienced a traumatic event.

EMDR therapy differs from other trauma treatments in several ways. Unlike traditional talk therapies, EMDR therapy involves bilateral stimulation, which helps to activate the brain’s natural healing processes. The therapist also allows the individual to integrate positive and adaptive beliefs about themselves and the world into the reprocessed memory. This approach is designed to help individuals reprocess traumatic memories more adaptively, leading to lasting symptom reduction.

Another critical difference between EMDR therapy and other trauma treatments is the speed at which it can produce results. While traditional talk therapies can take months or even years to improve significantly, EMDR therapy can produce results in as few as six to 12 sessions.

Despite the growing body of scientific evidence supporting EMDR therapy, there are still some controversies surrounding this therapy. One of the main criticisms of EMDR therapy is that the mechanism of action is not fully understood. While some have suggested that bilateral stimulation helps to activate the brain’s natural healing processes, others have argued that the available evidence does not support this explanation.
